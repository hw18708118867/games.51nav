## About the Stroop Test

The Stroop effect, discovered by John Ridley Stroop in 1935, is one of the most famous and widely replicated findings in cognitive psychology. It reveals a fundamental truth about how our brains process information: automatic processes (like reading) can interfere with controlled processes (like naming colors).

### The Classic Experiment

In Stroop's original experiment, participants were shown color words printed in incongruent ink colors (e.g., "RED" printed in blue ink). When asked to name the ink color, participants were significantly slower and made more errors compared to naming the ink color of neutral stimuli. This interference occurs because reading is so automatic that we can't easily suppress it.

### What It Measures

The Stroop test measures cognitive control — your brain's ability to override an automatic response in favor of a less automatic one. This ability is a key component of executive function and is associated with the prefrontal cortex. Strong Stroop performance correlates with better self-control, decision-making, and focus in daily life.
